Atrial Abnormalities

🫀
Left Atrial Abnormalities (LAA)
Introduction & Definition

Left atrial abnormalities refer to structural and electrical changes in the left atrium, commonly due to pressure or volume overload. They are frequently detected on ECG and echocardiography. Definition: Enlargement and/or delayed activation of the left atrium, usually secondary to chronic cardiac conditions.

Causes
  • Pressure overload: Mitral stenosis, Systemic hypertension, Aortic stenosis
  • Volume overload: Mitral regurgitation, Left ventricular failure
  • Other: Atrial fibrillation, Cardiomyopathy, Ischemic heart disease
Types
  • Left Atrial Enlargement (LAE)
  • Left Atrial Abnormality (Electrical changes without gross enlargement)
⚡ ECG Features
  • P mitrale — broad, notched P wave
  • P wave duration > 120 ms
  • Notched P wave in Lead II
  • Biphasic P wave in V1 — deep negative terminal component
Echocardiographic Findings
  • Increased LA diameter (>40 mm in adults)
  • Increased LA volume index (>34 mL/m²)
  • Dilated LA chamber
Clinical Significance
  • Risk of atrial fibrillation
  • Risk of thromboembolism (stroke)
  • Marker of chronic cardiac disease
  • Indicates diastolic dysfunction
  • Strong predictor of cardiovascular morbidity
🫀
Right Atrial Abnormalities (RAA)
Introduction & Definition

Right atrial abnormalities occur due to conditions causing increased pressure or volume in the right atrium. Definition: Enlargement or hypertrophy of the right atrium, affecting electrical activity and cardiac function.

Causes
  • Pressure overload: Pulmonary hypertension, Pulmonary stenosis
  • Volume overload: Tricuspid regurgitation, ASD
  • Other: Chronic lung disease (Cor pulmonale), Congenital heart diseases
Types
  • Right Atrial Enlargement (RAE)
  • Right Atrial Hypertrophy (rare, usually enlargement predominates)
⚡ ECG Features
  • P pulmonale — tall peaked P wave
  • P wave amplitude > 2.5 mm in Lead II
  • Tall P waves in inferior leads (II, III, aVF)
  • Positive peaked P wave in V1
Echocardiographic Findings
  • Enlarged RA area (>18 cm²)
  • Dilated right atrium
  • Associated RV enlargement
Clinical Significance
  • Indicates pulmonary or right heart disease
  • Associated with right heart failure
  • May lead to arrhythmias

Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH)

💪
LVH — Definition, Causes, ECG, Treatment & Significance
Definition

Increase in the muscle mass and wall thickness of the left ventricle due to chronic pressure or volume overload.

Causes
  • Pressure overload (most common): Systemic hypertension ⭐, Aortic stenosis, Coarctation of aorta
  • Volume overload: Aortic regurgitation, Mitral regurgitation
  • Other: Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, Athletic heart (physiological), Chronic kidney disease
⚡ ECG Features (Very Important)
  • 1. Sokolow–Lyon Criteria: S(V1) + R(V5/V6) ≥ 35 mm
  • 2. Other voltage: R in V5 or V6 ≥ 25 mm; R in aVL ≥ 11 mm
  • 3. Left Axis Deviation (LAD): QRS axis −30° to −90°
  • 4. ST-T Strain pattern: ST depression + T wave inversion in I, aVL, V5, V6
  • 5. QRS changes: Slightly prolonged; delayed intrinsicoid deflection in V5/V6 (>50 ms)
  • 6. Left Atrial Enlargement (associated): Broad, notched P wave
Treatment
  • Treat underlying cause: Hypertension → ACE inhibitors/ARBs/beta-blockers; Aortic stenosis → Valve replacement
  • Medical: ACE inhibitors/ARBs (reduce remodeling), Beta-blockers (reduce workload), Diuretics
  • Lifestyle: Salt restriction, Weight control, Moderate exercise
Clinical Significance
  • Predictor of cardiovascular morbidity and mortality
  • Leads to: Diastolic dysfunction, Heart failure, Arrhythmias (ventricular)
  • Risk of: Myocardial ischemia, Sudden cardiac death
  • ECG is screening; Echo is gold standard (confirmatory)
03

Left Ventricular Enlargement (LVE)

🔴
LVE — Definition, Causes, ECG, Treatment & Significance
Definition

Dilatation of the left ventricular cavity, often associated with increased volume load and sometimes with hypertrophy.

Causes
  • Volume overload (most common): Aortic regurgitation ⭐, Mitral regurgitation, VSD
  • Pressure overload (chronic): Systemic hypertension, Aortic stenosis
  • Myocardial diseases: Dilated cardiomyopathy, Ischemic heart disease (post-MI)
  • Other: Chronic anemia, Athletic heart
⚡ ECG Features (In Detail)
  • 1. Sokolow–Lyon Criteria: S(V1) + R(V5/V6) ≥ 35 mm
  • 2. Left Axis Deviation
  • 3. QRS: Slight widening; delayed intrinsicoid deflection V5/V6 (>50 ms)
  • 4. ST-T Strain: ST depression in I, aVL, V5, V6; T wave inversion
  • 5. Associated LAE: Broad, notched P waves
  • 6. Poor R wave progression (especially dilated cardiomyopathy)
  • 7. Possible arrhythmias: Ventricular ectopics, AF
⚠️ Exam Point

ECG cannot reliably differentiate LVH vs LVE. Echocardiography is required — shows increased LV internal diameter and assesses ejection fraction.

Treatment
  • Treat underlying cause (valve repair, antihypertensives, cardiomyopathy management)
  • ACE inhibitors/ARBs, Beta-blockers, Diuretics
  • Advanced: ICD/CRT in severe cases, Heart failure protocols

Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH)

💙
RVH — Definition, Causes, ECG, Treatment & Significance
Definition

Thickening of the right ventricular myocardium due to chronic pressure overload on the right ventricle.

Causes
  • Pressure overload (most common): Pulmonary hypertension ⭐, Pulmonary stenosis, Cor pulmonale
  • Congenital: Tetralogy of Fallot, VSD (late), ASD
  • Other: Tricuspid valve disease, Chronic PE
⚡ ECG Features (Very Important)
  • 1. Right Axis Deviation (RAD): QRS axis > +90° (key feature)
  • 2. Dominant R in V1: R/S > 1; Tall R > 7 mm
  • 3. Deep S in V5, V6
  • 4. RV strain pattern: ST depression & T inversion in V1–V3, inferior leads
  • 5. P pulmonale: Peaked P > 2.5 mm in Lead II
  • 6. QRS: Normal or incomplete/complete RBBB
  • 7. qR pattern in V1 (severe RVH)
Summary Classic Pattern
Right axis deviation Tall R in V1 Deep S in V6 RV strain pattern
Treatment
  • Pulmonary hypertension → Vasodilators, Oxygen
  • Lung disease → Bronchodilators, Steroids
  • Pulmonary stenosis → Balloon valvuloplasty/surgery
  • Diuretics, Anticoagulants (PE risk)

Right Ventricular Enlargement (RVE)

🔵
RVE — Definition, Causes, ECG, Treatment & Significance
Definition

Dilatation of the right ventricular cavity due to chronic volume or pressure overload. May occur with or without hypertrophy.

Causes
  • Volume overload: ASD ⭐, Tricuspid regurgitation, Pulmonary regurgitation
  • Pressure overload: Pulmonary hypertension, Pulmonary stenosis, Cor pulmonale
  • Other: Chronic PE, RV infarction, Congenital heart disease
⚡ ECG Features
  • 1. Right Axis Deviation (RAD)
  • 2. R/S > 1 in V1; R/S < 1 in V6
  • 3. Dominant R in V1 (>7 mm)
  • 4. Deep S in V5, V6
  • 5. RV strain: ST depression & T inversion in V1–V3, inferior leads
  • 6. P pulmonale (associated RAE)
  • 7. Incomplete/complete RBBB — rSR′ in V1
Treatment
  • ASD → Device closure or surgery
  • Tricuspid regurgitation → Valve repair
  • Pulmonary hypertension → Vasodilators
  • Diuretics, Oxygen therapy, Anticoagulants

Atrioventricular (AV) Blocks

⚡
AV Blocks — Overview, Definition & Causes
Definition

AV block is delay or complete interruption of impulse conduction from atria to ventricles, usually at the AV node or His–Purkinje system.

Causes
  • Physiological: Increased vagal tone (athletes, sleep)
  • Cardiac: Ischemic heart disease/MI ⭐, Degenerative conduction disease, Myocarditis, Cardiomyopathy
  • Drug-induced: Beta-blockers, Calcium channel blockers (verapamil), Digoxin
  • Metabolic: Hyperkalemia, Hypothyroidism
  • Congenital: Congenital AV block, Structural defects
1°
First-Degree AV Block
Definition

Delayed conduction through AV node but all impulses are conducted — no missed beats.

⚡ ECG Features
  • PR interval > 200 ms (0.20 sec) — prolonged and constant
  • Every P wave followed by a QRS (1:1 conduction)
  • Regular rhythm; Normal QRS; Normal P waves
Classic Pattern

"Prolonged, constant PR interval with no dropped QRS complexes"

Treatment
  • Usually no treatment required — often benign and asymptomatic
  • Stop/adjust causative drugs (beta-blockers, digoxin)
  • Correct electrolyte imbalance; Treat ischemia
  • Pacemaker rarely required
Clinical Significance
  • Common in athletes (high vagal tone) and elderly
  • Early sign of conduction system disease; may progress (rare)
  • Often incidental finding on ECG
2°
Second-Degree AV Block
Definition

Some atrial impulses fail to conduct to the ventricles, resulting in intermittent dropped QRS complexes.

Mobitz Type I (Wenckebach) — ECG Features
⚡ Type I ECG
  • Progressive PR prolongation with each beat
  • Dropped QRS — P wave not followed by QRS
  • Cycle repeats (grouped beating), Irregular rhythm
  • QRS usually normal (block at AV node)
Classic Pattern

"Longer, longer, longer… drop!"

Mobitz Type II — ECG Features
⚡ Type II ECG
  • Constant PR interval (fixed, no warning)
  • Sudden dropped QRS — without prior prolongation
  • Fixed ratios possible (2:1, 3:1 block)
  • QRS often wide (His–Purkinje disease)
Classic Pattern

"Normal PR… suddenly drop QRS"

  • Mobitz I: Usually no treatment; Atropine if symptomatic
  • Mobitz II ⭐: Permanent pacemaker; Temporary pacing if unstable

Clinical Significance
  • Mobitz I: Benign; seen in increased vagal tone, inferior MI
  • Mobitz II ⭐: High risk of progression to 3rd-degree block; syncope; sudden cardiac death
3°
Third-Degree AV Block (Complete Heart Block)
Definition

Complete failure of conduction between atria and ventricles — no atrial impulses reach ventricles. Atria and ventricles beat independently (AV dissociation).

⚡ ECG Features
  • AV Dissociation ⭐: P waves and QRS complexes have no relationship
  • Regular P–P; Atrial rate normal (60–100/min)
  • Regular R–R (escape rhythm); Ventricular rate very slow (20–40/min)
  • Variable PR interval (no fixed PR)
  • Narrow QRS → junctional escape (better prognosis)
  • Wide QRS → ventricular escape (worse prognosis)
  • More P waves than QRS complexes
Classic Pattern

"P waves marching through independently of QRS"

Treatment
  • Emergency: Temporary pacing (transcutaneous/transvenous); Atropine (limited)
  • Definitive: Permanent pacemaker (treatment of choice)
  • Treat underlying cause: MI reperfusion, Stop drugs, Correct electrolytes
🚨 Life-Threatening Condition

Causes severe bradycardia, reduced cardiac output. Symptoms: Stokes-Adams attacks (syncope), Dizziness, Fatigue, Heart failure. Always requires pacemaker.

Bundle Branch Blocks (BBB)

→
Right Bundle Branch Block (RBBB)
Definition

Conduction through the right bundle branch is delayed or blocked — right ventricle depolarizes later than the left.

Causes
  • Right ventricular hypertrophy
  • Pulmonary embolism ⭐
  • Chronic lung disease (cor pulmonale)
  • Congenital heart disease (e.g., ASD)
  • Ischemic heart disease; Can be normal variant
⚡ ECG Features
  • QRS ≥ 120 ms
  • rsR′ pattern in V1 — "M-shaped" / "rabbit ears"
  • Wide S wave in lateral leads (I, V5, V6)
  • T wave inversion in V1–V3
Treatment & Significance
  • Usually no specific treatment; Treat underlying cause
  • Often benign; May indicate right heart or pulmonary disease
  • New RBBB in chest pain → suspect pulmonary embolism
←
Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB)
Definition

Conduction through the left bundle branch is blocked — delayed depolarization of the left ventricle.

Causes
  • Hypertension ⭐
  • Ischemic heart disease
  • Aortic stenosis
  • Cardiomyopathy
  • Degenerative conduction disease
⚡ ECG Features
  • QRS ≥ 120 ms
  • Broad, notched R waves in I, aVL, V5, V6 ("M-shaped" in lateral)
  • No Q waves in lateral leads
  • Deep S / "W-shaped" in V1
  • ST depression + T inversion in lateral leads (discordant)
⭐ Always Pathological

LBBB indicates structural heart disease. Makes MI diagnosis difficult on ECG. Associated with poor prognosis and heart failure.

Treatment
  • Treat underlying cause
  • CRT (cardiac resynchronization) in heart failure
  • Pacemaker in severe conduction disease
½
Hemibundle Branch Block (Fascicular Block)
Definition

Block of one fascicle of the left bundle branch: Left anterior fascicle or Left posterior fascicle.

Causes
  • Ischemic heart disease
  • Hypertension
  • Degenerative fibrosis
  • Cardiomyopathy
⚡ LAFB ECG Features
  • Left axis deviation (−45° to −90°)
  • Small Q in I, aVL
  • Small R in II, III, aVF
⚡ LPFB ECG Features
  • Right axis deviation (> +90°)
  • Small R in I, aVL
  • Small Q in II, III, aVF
Clinical Significance
  • May be isolated or combined with other blocks
  • Can progress to complete heart block
  • QRS duration usually normal or slightly prolonged

Electrolyte Imbalance

⚗️
Electrolyte Imbalance — Overview
Definition

Abnormal levels of electrolytes in the blood (mainly K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Na⁺) that affect cardiac electrical activity, producing characteristic ECG changes.

Causes
  • Renal: CKD, Renal failure
  • Drug-induced: Diuretics, ACE inhibitors, Digoxin
  • GI loss: Vomiting, Diarrhea
  • Endocrine: Hyperaldosteronism, Hypoparathyroidism
  • Other: Dehydration, Severe illness, Burns
K⁺
Potassium Imbalance ⭐ (Most Important)
⚡ HYPERKALEMIA (↑ K⁺) — ECG (Progressive)
  • Tall, peaked T waves ("tent-shaped") — earliest sign
  • Flattened or absent P waves
  • Wide QRS complex
  • Sine wave pattern (severe)
  • Ventricular arrhythmias / cardiac arrest
⚡ HYPOKALEMIA (↓ K⁺) — ECG
  • Flattened T waves
  • Prominent U waves ⭐ (classic sign)
  • ST depression
  • Prolonged QT interval (apparent)
Treatment
  • Hyperkalemia (Emergency): Calcium gluconate (stabilize), Insulin + glucose (shift K⁺ in), Diuretics/Dialysis
  • Hypokalemia: K⁺ replacement (oral/IV)
Ca²⁺
Calcium & Magnesium Imbalance
⚡ ECG Changes
  • Hypercalcemia (↑ Ca²⁺): Short QT interval
  • Hypocalcemia (↓ Ca²⁺): Prolonged QT interval
  • Hypomagnesemia: Prolonged QT; Risk of torsades de pointes
  • Hypermagnesemia: Bradycardia; Prolonged PR and QRS
Treatment
  • Hypercalcemia: IV fluids, Diuretics, Bisphosphonates
  • Hypocalcemia: Calcium supplementation
  • Low Mg: Mg replacement; High Mg: Stop Mg intake

↑ K⁺Peaked T waves, wide QRS
↓ K⁺U wave, flat T wave

Metabolic Abnormalities

🧪
Metabolic Abnormalities — ECG Changes
Definition

Disturbances in the body's biochemical processes (acid–base, hormones, temperature, glucose) that affect cardiac electrophysiology and produce characteristic ECG changes.

Causes
  • Metabolic acidosis/alkalosis
  • Hypothyroidism / Hyperthyroidism
  • Hypothermia / Hyperthermia
  • Hypoglycemia / Hyperglycemia
  • Sepsis, Liver failure, Renal failure
⚡ ECG Features by Condition
  • Metabolic acidosis: Tachycardia; Hyperkalemia-like changes (peaked T, wide QRS)
  • Metabolic alkalosis: Hypokalemia-like (flat T, U waves, arrhythmias)
  • Hypothyroidism: Sinus bradycardia; Low voltage QRS; Prolonged QT; Flat/inverted T
  • Hyperthyroidism: Sinus tachycardia ⭐; Atrial fibrillation; Increased QRS voltage
  • Hypothermia: Osborn (J) wave ⭐ (pathognomonic); Bradycardia; Prolonged PR, QRS, QT
  • Hyperthermia: Sinus tachycardia; Arrhythmias
  • Hypoglycemia: Prolonged QT; Risk of arrhythmias
  • Sepsis/Systemic illness: Sinus tachycardia; Non-specific ST-T changes
Treatment
  • Treat underlying cause (correct acid-base, manage endocrine disorder)
  • Normalize body temperature; Control glucose
  • Hypothyroidism → Thyroxine; Hyperthyroidism → Antithyroid drugs
  • Hypothermia → Gradual rewarming
  • Supportive: IV fluids, Oxygen, ECG monitoring

Very Short Answer Q&A (2 Marks)

Q.28
State the normal duration and amplitude of the QRS complex.
Duration: 0.06–0.10 sec (≤ 100 ms). Amplitude: 5–30 mm (varies with leads). Represents ventricular depolarization.
Q.29
List ECG features of ventricular tachycardia.
Wide QRS complex (>120 ms); Rate 100–250 bpm; AV dissociation; Fusion and capture beats; Regular rhythm.
Q.30
Define PR interval and state its normal duration.
Definition: Time from start of P wave to start of QRS complex; represents atrial depolarization + AV conduction. Normal: 0.12–0.20 sec.
Q.31
List methods of estimating heart rate from ECG.
  • 300 rule: 300 ÷ large squares between R–R
  • 1500 rule: 1500 ÷ small squares between R–R
  • 6-second method: Count QRS in 6 sec × 10
  • 10-second method: Count beats × 6
Q.32
What is the pacemaker potential of the SA node?
Slow spontaneous depolarization during phase 4 due to inward Na⁺ and Ca²⁺ currents. Responsible for automaticity of the SA node.
Q.33
What is the classic ECG feature of atrial flutter?
Saw-tooth (F waves) ⭐ — best seen in leads II, III, aVF. Atrial rate ~250–350 bpm.
Q.34
Explain the significance of pathological Q waves.
Indicates myocardial infarction (old or acute) and represents myocardial necrosis. Criteria: Width > 0.04 sec; Depth > 25% of R wave amplitude.
Q.35
List ECG features of anterior wall myocardial infarction.
ST elevation in V1–V4 ⭐; Pathological Q waves in anterior leads; T wave inversion. Indicates LAD artery involvement.
Q.36
Define the AV node.
A specialized conduction tissue located in the interatrial septum near the tricuspid valve. Delays impulse transmission before passing to the ventricles.
Q.37
What is a premature ventricular contraction (PVC)?
Early ventricular beat arising from an ectopic focus. Features: Wide QRS; No preceding P wave; Compensatory pause.
Q.38
What is the ECG feature of dextrocardia?
Inverted P and QRS in Lead I; Right axis deviation; Poor R wave progression in chest leads.
Q.39
List ECG criteria for left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH).
  • High QRS voltage (Sokolow–Lyon: S(V1) + R(V5/V6) ≥ 35 mm)
  • Left axis deviation
  • Strain pattern (ST depression, T inversion in lateral leads)
  • Tall R in V5/V6; Deep S in V1
Q.40
Define significant ST elevation and ST depression.
ST Elevation: ≥ 1 mm in limb leads; ≥ 2 mm in chest leads.
ST Depression: ≥ 0.5 mm horizontal or downsloping.
Q.41
What is a hemifascicular block?
Block of one fascicle of the left bundle branch. Types: Left Anterior Fascicular Block (LAFB) and Left Posterior Fascicular Block (LPFB).
Q.42
State the normal amplitude and duration of T wave.
Duration: ~0.10–0.25 sec. Amplitude: <5 mm in limb leads; <10 mm in chest leads.
Q.43
What is the Sokolow–Lyon criteria for LVH?
S(V1) + R(V5/V6) ≥ 35 mm

If this voltage criterion is met, LVH is diagnosed. Confirm with echocardiography (gold standard).
Q.44
What is false negative TMT?
TMT (Treadmill Test) is normal despite presence of coronary artery disease. Causes: Inadequate exercise achieved; Single-vessel disease; Medications (beta-blockers blunting heart rate response).
